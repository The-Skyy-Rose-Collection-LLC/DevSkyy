CRITICAL CREATIVE DIRECTION — NATURAL PRODUCT PLACEMENT:

Stop placing products as floating hotspots in 3D space. Products need NATURAL HABITATS within each scene. Think of it like set design for a luxury fashion editorial shoot.

Examples of what we mean:
- A leather jacket draped over a wrought-iron garden bench in the Black Rose cathedral garden
- Silk blouses hanging on ornate vintage dress racks in the Ballroom
- Shoes placed on marble pedestals or stone steps along the cathedral path
- A handbag sitting on a velvet chaise lounge
- Rings and jewelry displayed on antique vanity mirrors or inside glass bell jars
- A dress hanging from a chandelier chain or draped over a balcony railing
- Streetwear pieces laid across the hood of a car on the Runway scene

HOW TO BUILD THIS:
1. Create FURNITURE / PROP LAYERS in each Three.js scene — racks, tables, pedestals, mannequins, benches, display cases
2. Products attach TO these props, not floating in air
3. Each collection room needs its own set of contextual props that match its theme:
   - BLACK ROSE (cathedral garden): stone benches, iron gates, rose arbors, vintage mirrors
   - LOVE HURTS (ballroom): velvet furniture, chandeliers, candelabras, ornate frames
   - SIGNATURE (runway/city): industrial racks, neon signs, concrete pedestals, glass displays
4. The hotspot glow/interaction still works — but the click target is the product ON the prop, not a floating orb
5. Add subtle environmental animations to props (candle flicker, fabric sway, light shimmer)

This is what separates a luxury 3D experience from a generic product viewer. The products should look like they BELONG in the world, like you stumbled into a styled editorial set.
